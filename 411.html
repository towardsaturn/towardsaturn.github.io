411 Seniors Centre Logo and Website

This case study is based off of a project that I was tasked with while working at 411 Seniors Centre in 2016.
I had been asked to redesign the logo, and acquire feedback surrounding the client‚Äôs existing website. Leading this project, I was responsible for user research, visual design, and developing wireframes and prototypes. 		Date

Role

Challenge

My challenge was to redesign a logo, and determine user's likes and dislikes about the current website.

In order to do so, I set out to:
1. Gather data on the thoughts and opinions of the users regarding the orignal logo (see below)
2. Listen to what the centre users wanted in the new logo
3. User thoughts on the existing website
4. Based on the above, redesign a logo and create wireframes



The original logo design.


Process

The first step was to gather quantitative and qualitative data regarding the current logo and website. I created an anonymous online survey and sent it out to the mailing list comprised of 231 users. To avoid selection-bias, in person interviews were also conducted with users who did not have access to e-mail or the internet. In total, 42 participants responded.

Example Questions:

1. Do you believe that 411's current branding reflects its objectives well?
2. What are your thoughts on our current logo?
3. What elements would you like to see in our new logo? Provide key words. E.g.: Dynamic, Reliable, Business-like etc.

Data was regarding the website was also surveyed. The results showcased that users wanted smaller, less intimidating images, larger font, more colors, and content about activism.

While mindful of the intended audience (the older folks!), as well as the survey responses, I aimed to create a simple, clean layout. Key points include less intimidating photos (previously of extremely close up face shots), no extremely light fonts, etc.

I also changed the ‚ÄúJoin us!‚Äù page in the navigation, to ‚ÄúMembership‚Äù as per one users‚Äô request. And on the actual membership page,
I resized and organized the boxes for the sign-up form to make better use of white space. Furthermore, under the ‚ÄúWhat services are you interested in‚Äù, I directly listed examples of offered services (such as yoga classes, information and referral services, etc.), allowing potential members to select as many boxes as they‚Äôd like. Listing all the services offered could also pull further interest and introduce potential members to programs that they may not have previously been aware of.

Compiling all my user-research and results into a powerpoint presentation, I pitched my findings and suggestions to the Executive Director. I was met with extremely positive feedback, and the executive director kept my data on hand so that they could utilize it in the near future.

Limitations that I encountered were that although the centre supposedly has 432 members, only 42 surveys were collected. In-person interviews (3) were limited, and could have been influenced by interviewer bias. There was also the possibility that members perceived, ‚ÄúOkay‚Äù as interchangeable with ‚ÄúGood‚Äù when rating the website. In-person participants had the ability to ask for further clarification whereas those who did the online survey did not.
